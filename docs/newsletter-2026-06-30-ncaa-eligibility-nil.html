The Clock, The Money, And What You Control

If you follow college sports at all, your feed caught fire this week. The NCAA changed how long players can compete, everyone started yelling about the transfer portal, and somewhere in the noise those three letters showed up again: NIL. Let me cut through it in about five minutes. Two of these things touch your house, and one of them is completely in your hands.
 
First, The Clock

Start with the rule everyone is reacting to. For decades a college athlete had five years to play four seasons, with a redshirt year to lean on. As of June 23, that is gone. Division I now runs an age-based model. Your player gets five seasons of competition inside a five-year window, and the clock starts at the earlier of two things: full-time college enrollment, or the academic year after they turn 19.

Sit with that second one. The clock can start on age, before your player ever picks a school. So a reclass year, a prep year, or a JUCO stop that pushes enrollment past the year they turn 19 can quietly burn a season. And the cushion is thinner now, because the medical redshirt is gone. An injury no longer pauses anything. The only things that stop the clock are a religious mission, maternity, or active-duty military service.

Before you panic, two things the viral posts leave out. This is not a switch that flips this fall. Players already in college and this fall's freshmen get whichever set of rules helps them more, so nobody loses a year overnight. The strict version only hits players who first enroll in fall 2027 and later. And this is a Division I rule. Division II and III are not changing.
 
Then, The Money

So why are players fighting this hard to hang on to every season? In a word, money. Which brings us to NIL, the thing everyone references and almost nobody explains. Here it is in plain English.

NIL stands for name, image, and likeness. It simply means an athlete is allowed to get paid for their own brand, the same way any person can be paid to endorse something. A local business pays them to post. A camp pays them to show up. A card company pays for an autograph. That is NIL. It is not a paycheck for playing.

Here is the genuinely new part. At the college level, a court settlement now lets schools pay athletes directly too, through revenue sharing, capped around 20 million dollars per school and climbing about four percent a year. That is a separate bucket from outside NIL deals, and the bigger deals now run through a clearinghouse that checks them for fair market value. The whole system is still being fought over in court, so expect it to keep shifting.
 
What This Means For A High School Family

Now the part that matters for a high school family, and I am going to be straight with you. The big money lives in football and men's basketball. At a major program those two sports can eat ninety percent of the pool. Baseball and softball get a sliver, often a few thousand dollars, often nothing. So if anyone is selling your 14 year old a future of NIL checks, hold onto your wallet.

High school NIL does exist though. More than 40 states allow it now, California included. But California's rules are strict, and breaking them costs eligibility, so know them. The deal has to be independent of the school. No team uniform, logo, or mascot in it. No coach, no booster, and no school arranging it for you. No pay-for-play, and nothing in the banned categories like alcohol, vaping, gambling, or firearms. One careless post can sideline a good player.

So here is the honest map. The clock is new, the NIL world is loud, and most of it sits outside your control. What is inside your control did not change at all this week: grades, skills, exposure, and a clean recruiting path. None of these rule changes touched the academic side. The core-course GPA and the Eligibility Center certification your player needs to be a qualifier are exactly the same as last week. That is still the gate. A 95 mph arm with a core GPA under the line does not get cleared, NIL or not.
 
Coach's Take

After 35 years in baseball as a coach, a trainer, a dad, and a mentor, I have watched a lot of families chase the shiny thing and miss the lever right in front of them. You cannot control a council vote in Indianapolis, a lawsuit in Tennessee, or whether a brand ever calls your kid.

You can control whether your player knows their core GPA in October instead of finding out in May of senior year that they came up half a grade short. The rules will keep changing. The work that actually moves a player forward does not.
