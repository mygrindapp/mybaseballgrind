Your Player Gets One Throwing Arm

It is late July. The spring season, the summer tournaments, and a few hundred throws nobody counted are all sitting in your player's arm right now, and fall signups are already open. This is the week to talk about arm care, before the next season starts, not after the elbow starts talking first.

And arm care is not one conversation. It changes as your player grows. The 10 year old, the varsity sophomore, and the college freshman carry the same arm through three completely different worlds. So this issue walks the whole ladder: the youth arm, the high school arm, and the college arm, and what actually protects each one.
 
The Youth Arm (8 to 13): It Is a Growth Plate, Not a Ligament

A young arm is not a small adult arm. Until the growth plates close, the weakest link in the elbow is not the ligament the pros tear. It is the growth plate itself, and the injury every parent should know by name is Little League elbow. The warning signs: pain on the inside of the elbow, accuracy or velocity quietly dropping, a kid shaking their arm out between pitches, or the classic "it only hurts when I throw hard." That sentence sounds reassuring. It is the opposite.

At this age the protections are counting rules, and they belong to you: Pitch Smart limits (about 50 pitches a day at 7 to 8, 75 at 9 to 10, 85 at 11 to 12), counted across every team, lesson, and backyard session combined, because arms fail on the total. No pitching and catching the same day. 2 to 3 months a year with no overhead throwing at all. And the family rule that protects everything: arm pain always gets a doctor, never gets pitched through. Caught early, this heals with rest. Softball families, the windmill being easier on the elbow does not make your pitcher injury-proof, and softball has no universal pitch counts. You are the pitch counter.

The High School Arm (14 to 18): The Danger Years

This is where the injury numbers actually spike. The fastest growing group of Tommy John surgery patients is not major leaguers. It is teenagers. The reasons stack quietly: velocity suddenly matters, radar guns show up, showcases sell exposure, and your player may be throwing for the school team, a travel team, and a lesson coach in the same month, with each one seeing only his slice. Pitch Smart still applies here (95 a day at 13 to 16, 105 at 17 to 18, with rest days that scale), but at this age the chart is not the problem. The uncounted total is. Fatigue is the loudest injury signal researchers have found, and never pitching a tired arm matters more now than ever.

Two more things every high school family needs said out loud. First, the myth: Tommy John surgery does not make anyone throw harder. The velocity some kids come back with was built by a year of structured rehab and a growth spurt, not the scalpel. The surgery is a 12 to 18 month detour with no guaranteed return. Second, honesty gets harder at exactly the moment it matters most. Playing time and recruiting are on the line, so a sore 16 year old says nothing. Make the family deal explicit: telling the truth about the arm never costs anything at home.
 
The College Arm: Durability Is a Recruiting Tool

Here is what most families learn too late: college coaches do not just recruit velocity. They recruit arms that will survive a college workload, because fall innings plus a full spring season break arms that arrive with hidden mileage. And once your player is on campus, nobody counts for them. The strength staff hands them a routine, and owning it is part of the job. The players who last are the ones who showed up already owning one.

That is why the years before college count twice. A player who arrives with a real arm care habit, and a real history (what they threw, when they rested, how the arm responded) stands out in a way a radar gun cannot measure. I watched my own son climb from a small local high school to junior college to D2 All-American to draft day. The arms that survive that climb are rarely the hardest throwers at 15. They are the ones whose routine survived every level.
 
Coach's Take

In 35 years I have never seen an arm ruined on one bad day. Every one was ruined on a total nobody was adding up. Spring coach saw his slice. Summer coach saw his. The lesson guy saw a bullpen a week. The kid felt the fatigue and said nothing, because kids always say nothing when playing time is on the line.

That is why the most protective piece of equipment your family owns might be the log, and the log grows up with your player. At 10, you count together on the ride home. In high school, they log it themselves and the pattern is sitting there when you ask how the arm feels. By college, the habit rides along with them, and it is the difference. When every outing goes in MyGrind, with pitches thrown and how the arm felt after, the season total stops being a guess, and the app's arm care checklist turns the boring band routine into a box your player actually likes checking. The record is how a loaded July gets caught before it becomes a lost spring.
