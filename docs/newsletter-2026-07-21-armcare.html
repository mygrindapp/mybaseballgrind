Your Player Gets One Throwing Arm

It is late July. The spring season, the summer tournaments, and a few hundred throws nobody counted are all sitting in your player's arm right now, and fall ball signups are already open. This is the week to talk about arm care, before the next season starts, not after the elbow starts talking first.

Here is the sentence that changes how families see this: a young arm is not a small adult arm. Until the growth plates close, the weakest link in the elbow is not the ligament the pros tear. It is the growth plate itself. That one fact is why kids get hurt differently, and why the fixes are rules and routines, not gadgets.
 
Little League Elbow Is a Growth Plate Injury

Bones grow at soft zones called growth plates, and in a young thrower the plate on the inside of the elbow takes the pull of every single throw. Overload it and you get the injury every parent should know by name: Little League elbow.

The warning signs: pain on the inside of the elbow when throwing, accuracy or velocity quietly dropping, a kid shaking their arm out between pitches, or the classic "it only hurts when I throw hard." That sentence sounds reassuring. It is the opposite.

The family rule that protects everything: arm pain always gets a doctor, and never gets pitched through. Caught early, this heals with rest. Pitched through, it can become casts and surgery. No July tournament is worth that trade. We are coaches, not doctors, and this is the one place we hand the ball off every time.
 
The Numbers That Do the Protecting

The Pitch Smart limits most leagues use: about 50 pitches a day at 7 to 8 years old, 75 at 9 to 10, 85 at 11 to 12, 95 at 13 to 16, and 105 at 17 to 18, with required rest days that scale with the count. Learn your league's chart. Then remember what the chart cannot see: the limits only work if they are counted across every team, every lesson, and every backyard session combined. Arms fail on the total.

Three more rules the sports medicine world agrees on: 2 to 3 months a year with no overhead throwing at all, no pitching and catching in the same day, and never pitch a tired arm. Fatigue is the loudest injury signal researchers have found. And softball families, the windmill being easier on the elbow does not make your pitcher injury-proof, and softball has no universal pitch counts. You are the pitch counter.
 
The Tommy John Myth, Retired

The fastest growing group of Tommy John surgery patients is not major leaguers. It is teenagers. And the myth that follows the surgery around every bleacher needs to go: Tommy John does not make anyone throw harder. The velocity some kids come back with was built by a year of structured rehab and a growth spurt, not the scalpel. The surgery itself is a 12 to 18 month detour with no guaranteed return.

Coach's Take

In 35 years I have never seen an arm ruined on one bad day. Every one was ruined on a total nobody was adding up. Spring coach saw his slice. Summer coach saw his. The lesson guy saw a bullpen a week. The kid felt the fatigue and said nothing, because kids always say nothing when playing time is on the line.
