Private School, Public School, or the Right School?

It is high school season right now for a different reason. Not games. Decisions. All over the country, eighth graders are touring high schools for the baseball or softball program, families are weighing the private powerhouse against the public school down the street, and group chats are full of who is transferring where. The section and the division get argued about like they decide a kid's future.

It is one of the most stressful calls a baseball or softball family makes, and it gets made fast, usually in the summer before everything locks in. So let's slow it down. Private or public, big section or small, stay or transfer. Here is an honest take on what actually matters, and the one thing most parents get backwards.
 
The Question Behind the Question

When a parent asks me which high school their kid should pick, there is almost always a quieter question underneath it. Sometimes it is fear: they are not sure their kid is good enough for the program they have their eye on. Sometimes it is the opposite: they are pretty sure their kid is better than the situation they are in. Either way, they are really asking the same thing: where does my kid have the best chance to develop and get seen.

My answer is the same for both. Go to the school that gives the best education and the best chance to actually be on the field. A trophy case tells you who a program recruited last year. It does not tell you whether your kid will ever get off the bench, and the bench is where exposure goes to die.
 
Private vs Public: What Actually Differs

Let's kill the myth that private always wins and public always loses, or the other way around. Both can be great. Both can be a mistake. The label is not the variable.

Private programs often have more resources and can draw players from a wider area, which can mean stronger competition. It also means more competition for your kid's spot, and a tuition bill. Public programs are free, rooted in your community, and plenty of them are powerhouses in their own right, especially with open enrollment in a lot of districts now.

The things that actually matter cut across both: Is the coach a developer or just a collector of talent? Who is on the roster ahead of your player, and will your player actually play? Are the academics right for them? Can your family really do the cost and the commute for four years? Answer those four honestly and the private versus public question mostly answers itself.
 
Sections, Divisions, and the Transfer Trap

Parents put a lot of weight on the section and the division. In California that is Southern Section versus City Section. In other states it is the big school versus small school classification, the parochial league, the open enrollment loophole. It matters less than the volume of the argument suggests.

Where it gets dangerous is the transfer. The move to the powerhouse to be seen is the most common trap in youth ball. Most state associations have transfer and eligibility rules, and many include a sit out period for an athletically motivated move, so step one is always to check your own state's rules before anyone falls in love with a new jersey.

But the real cost of transferring up is playing time. Chasing the bigger name often means less of it, stuck behind older committed kids. And playing time, it turns out, is the whole ballgame. Here is why.
 
Exposure Is Playing Time. Do the Math.

Here is the part that should make the decision easier. A school has two real jobs: give your kid a great education, and give them a real chance at exposure. And exposure is not the name on the jersey. It is playing time. If your player is on the field and they perform, the people who want them will find them. Recruiters follow production. They always have.

Now flip it. The kid at the higher level school who rides the bench loses on both ends. The exposure is gone, because nobody gets recruited sitting down, and they fall behind in the only thing that builds a player: reps. Here is the real math. A varsity season runs about 20 to 30 games. A hitter in the everyday lineup gets roughly three at bats a game, so call it 70 to 90 at bats in a season, plus well over a hundred innings in the field, or 40 to 60 innings on the mound for a pitcher in the rotation. A kid on the bench gets a small fraction of all of it. That is a gap of 60 to 80 at bats in a single season, and across a high school career it stacks into the hundreds. A spring showcase cannot hand those reps back.

And this is not an argument for the small school over the big one. If your kid can go play at the highest level and stay on the field, wonderful, do it. The rule is not big or small, or varsity or JV. The rule is play. A kid getting every rep on JV is often better off than a kid watching from the varsity bench. So whether a parent worries their kid is not good enough for a program, or quietly thinks their kid is too good for it, I give them the same answer. Go where they will get a great education and actually get on the field. The field is where players are made, and it is where they are found.
 
Coach's Take

Let me tell you about my own son, because he is the whole point. We did not leave the bigger school chasing anything. We moved out of the area, and making the drive back for a private school, on top of finding the tuition every year, just did not make sense. So he ended up at our local school. Smaller program, nothing fancy.

And that is where it happened. He played, he produced, and he helped that program win more games than it ever had. From there he went on to play junior college baseball, got recruited to a Division II school, became an All-American, and was ultimately drafted. None of that happened because he was at the best school. It happened because he got on the field, in a good league, against real competition, and he excelled. They found him because he was playing.

So I do not put much weight on the trophy case. I put it on experience and playing time. No one gets seen sitting down. One thing I have learned in 35 years is this: baseball and softball will put you exactly where you belong. Your only job is to make sure where you belong is on the field.
