The Slushie Is Not a Hydration Plan

It is July. Doubleheaders, showcase weekends, and practices that start at 4pm on turf that has been baking since noon. And most players are running all of it on a gas station slushie and whatever was left in the bag.

Here is the thing about fueling: it is the part of development nobody posts about. There is no highlight reel for a good breakfast. But 35 years of watching players wilt in the fifth inning of a July doubleheader will teach you that the flat days almost never start at the field. They start the night before, at the table and at the tap.

So this week, the three fueling questions every baseball and softball parent asks, answered straight: water, food, and the one about the supplement aisle.
 
Hydration Is a Day-Before Job

By the time your player feels thirsty at the field, they are already behind. Thirst is a lagging stat. In July heat, a kid can lose more water in one practice than they can comfortably drink back the same day, which means the game against dehydration is won the day before anyone puts on cleats.

The habit that works is boring and repeatable: water with every meal the day before, a full bottle in the hour before practice, and drinks at every break, not just when they remember. Sports drinks earn their place in long, hot sessions and doubleheaders. The rest of the time, water wins, and the neon stuff is just sugar with a marketing budget.

The simple check coaches have used forever: if your player is not using the bathroom normally on a hot day, they are not drinking enough. Old school, still undefeated.
 
The Best Pre-Game Meal Is Boring

Two to three hours before a game: real food, mostly carbs, some protein, nothing new. Chicken and rice. A turkey sandwich. Pasta. Game day is not the day to experiment, and it is definitely not the day to skip breakfast because the nerves showed up first.

In the dugout, keep it simple: fruit, granola bars, pretzels. Things that go down easy between innings and do not sit like a brick. The kid eating a giant burger between games of a doubleheader is about to play the second game in slow motion.

And do not let the postgame meal be the skipped one. After the game is when the rebuild happens: carbs and protein within an hour or so. Chocolate milk is still one of the best recovery drinks ever invented, and your player will not argue with it.
 
The Supplement Aisle Can Wait

Here is the honest answer to the question I get every summer: most young athletes do not need supplements. They need sleep, water, and real meals, and at 13 to 15 those basics will out-develop anything in a tub by a mile.

Some of it is worse than unnecessary. Powders with "proprietary blends" on the label, and pre-workouts marketed to teenagers, are a hard no. And the creatine question, the one your player has already heard about from an older teammate, deserves a real conversation with your pediatrician, not a locker-room recommendation. If your family ever does choose a supplement, look for NSF Certified for Sport on the label. We are coaches, not doctors, and anyone selling your 14-year-old a shortcut in a tub is neither.

Coach's Take

Every summer somebody asks me what the players who hold up in the heat are taking. The answer has never once been interesting. They went to bed. They ate breakfast. They showed up with a full jug and drank it. The kid who dies in the fifth inning of game two is almost never out of shape. They are out of fuel, and they have been since Thursday.

Here is the part that ties it together: fueling shows up in the log. When your player notes how they felt after each session in MyGrind, the pattern stops hiding. The flat Tuesday follows the 1am night. The strong Saturday follows the real breakfast. Before anyone spends a dollar in the supplement aisle, open the last month of the journal together and look. If the routine is not on the record, the routine is not real yet, and that is the honest place to start.
