What Should Your Player Be Doing This Summer?

The school season is over. The calendar suddenly looks empty, and that empty calendar is the most important stretch of your player's year. Ask ten families what to do with it and you will get ten different answers. Play more. Get seen. Train. Rest. Everybody is sure, and most are guessing.

It hits baseball and softball families the same way. The tournaments are stacking up, the showcase emails are landing, the trainer down the street has a summer package, and the group chat is full of where everyone else is committing. It is loud, it is expensive, and almost none of it comes with a way to know if it worked.

Here is the part nobody schedules around. Summers do not separate players because of how busy they were. They separate players because of how intentional they were. The kid who played sixty games and never took a real swing in the cage can fall behind the kid who played thirty and trained the other days. More is not the plan. The plan is the plan.

So let's make the summer simple. Four buckets, an honest take on showcases, the work that actually compounds, and the one thing every overscheduled family forgets to put on the calendar.
 
Play, Get Seen, or Get Better

Almost everything a family can do this summer falls into three buckets. The mistake is trying to fill all three at once and doing all three at half speed.

Play is games: travel ball, tournaments, summer league. It builds feel, competitiveness, and in game decisions you cannot get from a cage. Get seen is exposure: showcases, camps, recruiting events. It matters, but only at the right age and only when there is something worth showing. Get better is development: strength, skill work, arm care, conditioning. It is the least glamorous bucket and the one that compounds the most.

Pick the bucket that fits your player's age and goals, and let it lead. A 10 year old's summer should be almost all play and get better, with get seen barely on the radar. A rising junior's summer leans hard into get seen, built on a foundation of get better. Match the buckets to the player in front of you, not to the loudest family in the group chat.
 
More Games Is Not More Development

The easiest summer to sign up for is the one with the most games on it, and it is usually the one that develops your player the least. Sixty, seventy, eighty games in a summer sounds like commitment. What it often means is a tired arm, a swing nobody fixed, and a kid running on adrenaline and concession stand food.

Games are where you compete. They are not where you improve. Improvement happens in the reps between games, the ones with no scoreboard. A hitter does not fix anything in a Sunday bracket game, they just find out what is broken. The fixing happens Tuesday in the cage.

So before you commit to another tournament, ask one question: when does my player actually get better this week? If the honest answer is never, you have a schedule full of games and empty of development. Trade a few of those weekends for training weeks. The standings will not remember July. Your player's swing in September will.
 
Showcases: Worth It, and When

Showcase season runs hot in the summer and the marketing is relentless, so let's be honest about it. A showcase is a place to be measured and seen. That is valuable when two things are true: your player is at a recruiting age, generally the high school years, and they have something worth measuring.

Below that, most showcases are selling exposure a young player is not ready to use. Paying to put a 12 year old in front of college coaches who are not recruiting 12 year olds is paying for a feeling, not a result. One well chosen event for the experience does no harm. Spending the whole summer, and the whole budget, chasing exposure before there is anything to expose does real harm.

When your player is the right age, be strategic. One or two events that match their level beat a summer of expensive ones. Show up in shape, with numbers you have actually trained for, and a short list of schools that fit. Do not pay to be seen before your player is worth seeing. Spend the summer becoming worth seeing instead.
 
The Work That Actually Compounds

Strip away the games and the showcases and you are left with the bucket that quietly builds careers: getting better. It is not complicated, and it works the same for baseball and softball.

Strength and movement. Summer is the best window of the year to get stronger and move better, because nothing is competing with it. You do not need a fancy gym, you need consistency: a few real sessions a week, age appropriate, with an adult who knows what they are doing.

Arm care, every week. Baseball arms and softball arms both pay for a summer of throwing with no maintenance. Bands, a real warm up, and built in rest are not optional. The windmill motion and the overhand motion stress the body differently, so the care looks a little different, but the rule is the same: protect the arm now or pay for it in the fall.

Skill work with a purpose. Not random swings and random throws. A few things your player is actually trying to improve, worked on the days between games. Quality over volume, every time.

Rest, on the calendar. This is the one nobody schedules, and it is the one that prevents the August breakdown. Build in real days off and at least a couple of weeks where your player does not touch a ball. Burnout and overuse injuries are not bad luck, they are the predictable result of a summer with no off switch.
 
Coach's Take

The best summers my son ever had were not the busiest ones. They were the most intentional. There were summers we chased every tournament and every event, came home exhausted and broke, and could not point to a single thing he did better in the fall. And there were summers we played less, trained more, and protected his arm, and he showed up in September looking like a different player.

What changed was not the amount of work. It was knowing what the work was for. When a summer has a plan, every cage session and every lift and every rest day is pulling in the same direction. When it does not, you are just busy, and busy feels like progress until the season starts and the progress is not there.

You do not need the most expensive summer. You need an honest one. Pick the buckets that fit your player, protect the arm, schedule the rest, and keep some kind of record of what they actually did. Come fall, you want to look back and see a plan that was followed, not a calendar that was survived.
