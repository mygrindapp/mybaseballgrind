The Stat Your Kid's Memory Keeps Lying About

One at-bat is not the game. One game is not the weekend. And one weekend is not the player. But in the car ride home those lines blur, and they blur differently for everyone in the car.

It happens after a single game and it happens across a whole tournament. Your kid goes 2 for 3 with a walk and a hard out, a genuinely good game, but the at-bat they remember is the strikeout to end it, so they walk off convinced they were terrible. Or they go 1 for 9 over three games but smoked one ball in the last inning, and they leave the park feeling locked in. Same trap, different size. The last thing that happened paints over everything that came before it.

And it splits by who you are. The parent usually remembers the highlight, the laser in the third, the diving play in the gap. The player usually remembers the failure, the punchout, the one that got away with two runners on. Neither one is wrong. A parent holds the good because they are proud of their kid. A player holds the bad because it stung and they want it gone. Both memories are real, and both are completely human.

The problem is never the memory. The problem is what you decide to build on top of it. The same game, the same weekend, remembered two different ways, can send two families in opposite directions. This is about how to read it honestly, one at-bat, one game, or one tournament, so the decisions that follow are aimed at the right thing.
 
The Result Is Not the At-Bat

Start here, because everything else hangs on it: the result and the at-bat are not the same thing. In a single game a kid can go 0 for 3 and square the ball up all three times, scorched right at people. That is a good game the box score files under bad. Another kid bloops two off the end of the bat, beats out a swinging bunt, goes 3 for 3, and the box score calls it a great day.

If you coach the result, you fix the swing that was never broken and you praise the one that got lucky. The kid who hit three rockets walks away thinking they stink and starts tinkering with a swing that was working. The kid who blooped two starts believing the soft contact is the plan. One game can plant that wrong lesson, and a season of coaching results instead of at-bats waters it.

So separate the two questions every single time, after every game. Did my kid get a good result, and did my kid have a good at-bat? The first one is loud and the second one is true, and the second one is the one worth coaching.
 
Zoom Out Before You Panic

The second trap is sample size, and it works at every level. One bad at-bat is not a bad game. One bad game is not a bad tournament. One cold tournament is not a slump. A hitter going through a normal 2 for 15 stretch is not broken, they are a hitter, and hitters go through that. Even big leaguers hitting .300 fail seven times out of ten. The ups and downs are not the exception in this game. They are the game.

The fix is to zoom out one level before you react. Mad about an at-bat? Look at the game. Down on the game? Look at the week. Worried about the weekend? Look at the month. Almost every time you widen the frame, the crisis shrinks back into normal variance doing what variance does. The families who spiral are the ones reacting to the smallest possible sample: the last at-bat, the last game, the last bad feeling.

This is why keeping any kind of record changes how a family thinks. It does not have to be fancy. A note in your phone, a line in a notebook, or a logged entry in something like MyGrind, where the season builds itself game by game while your player just writes down what happened and how it actually felt. The tool matters less than the habit. The point is that when the panic comes, you have the bigger picture to look at instead of the loudest moment in your memory.
 
Coach's Take

My son taught me the best version of this without meaning to. He told me that after an 0 for 4 he would go hit, because he hated the feeling and wanted it gone. Then he said he would go hit after a 4 for 4 too, because he loved that feeling and did not want to lose it. Same work either way. He had figured out, on his own, how to use the bad game and the good game as the exact same fuel.

Sit with that, because it is the whole thing. He was not riding the result up and down, game to game. The result, good or bad, just pointed him back to the cage. That is the approach that separates the players who keep climbing from the ones who stall, and the way kids stall is the opposite mistake: the great game or the hot weekend that becomes a reason to coast. The good result feels like permission to relax, the edge dulls right when it felt sharpest, and a hot stretch turns into the most dangerous week of the season.

You cannot give a kid that mindset with a speech. But you can build the runway for it. When a player gets in the habit of writing down what they worked on and how the at-bats really felt, not just the line score, they slowly stop living and dying by any single game. The good day and the bad day both become information, and information sends you back to work. Good game or bad game, you log it, and you go hit.
